    <h1 class="hed" itemprop="headline">A Lovely Map of Phone Reception in the New York Subway</h1>
    <p class="dek" itemprop="description">Its creators probed the MTA&rsquo;s <span>660 miles of track with a prototype data logger.</span></p>

        <div><figure style="max-width: 605.0px;"><img alt="" src="https://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2016/02/d/6227d240f.jpg"></figure><p>A quintessential <a href="http://www.citylab.com/locations/cities/new-york/">New York</a> experience&#8212;maybe even more than eating a lox bagel or being ignored by an outer borough-hating taxi&#8212;is entering the subway and having your phone reception promptly drop into a black hole.</p>

<p>Yes, this most advanced of cities has extremely sucky train coverage. And while the MTA is <a href="http://www.mta.info/news-wi-fi-transit-wireless-subway/2015/11/12/start-next-phase-connect-37-stations-brooklyn-and">somewhere in the middle phase</a> of wiring 279 below-ground stations, there are still places you can&#8217;t make a call to save your life.</p>

<p>These coverage deserts and rare oases are the subject of a fascinating new endeavor from <a href="https://twitter.com/dgoddemeyer">Daniel Goddemeyer</a> and <a href="https://twitter.com/dominikus">Dominikus Baur</a>, who&#8217;ve made an <a href="http://www.subspotting-app.nyc/">iPhone app</a> and <a href="http://subspotting.nyc/main/#poster">MTA-licensed posters</a> illustrating the unequal geography of subway reception. &#8220;The Subspotting project captures, maps, and visualizes the available cell phone reception along all 21 lines, 469 stations, and 660 miles of track of the New York City Subway,&#8221; they write in a <a href="https://www.dropbox.com/sh/0pt1g7q0vro68bz/AAAEihXTI7jxdb0qyz2PtNn8a/ProjectDescription.docx?dl=0">press release</a>. &#8220;It is the first project of its kind to capture the cell phone reception in the entire subway system to provide New Yorkers &#8230; with information on where to make the next call or send the next text while underground.&#8221;</p>

<figure style="max-width: 620.0px;"><img alt="" src="https://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2016/02/n/3144b1af8.jpg"><figcaption class="credit"><a href="http://subspotting.nyc/main/#">Subspotting</a></figcaption></figure><p><span style="line-height: 1.52941;">The project got started when Goddemeyer, a researcher and designer at Brooklyn&#8217;s </span><a href="http://www.offc.co/web/index.php" style="line-height: 1.52941;">Object Form Field Culture</a><span style="line-height: 1.52941;">, noticed on his uptown commute little areas of connectivity that allowed him to finish emails underground. That led to an extensive poking-around of the train system, in which the Subspotting team used a prototype data logger to detect levels of reception for Verizon, </span><span style="line-height: 1.52941;">AT&amp;T, </span><span style="line-height: 1.52941;">T-Mobile, and Sprint.</span></p>

<p>The resulting app shows the quality of reception at stations on your line as different-sized bars. It also pinpoints places with wi-fi and colors blue stretches of track that are above ground. You can now use it (or the $40 posters) to plan where you&#8217;ll be able to call work, and roughly how much time you&#8217;ll have to tell the boss why another MTA delay is making you late.</p>

<p>In their expeditions, the Subspotting folks found the 7 and J lines had the most coverage along their complete length (at 79 and 75 percent, respectively), while the G&#8212;doomed to be abysmal at everything&#8212;had the least at 12 percent. Here are some other fun things they discovered:</p>

<blockquote>
<p><strong>Lots of spotty signals &#8211; The 1 train</strong></p>

<p>Spotty reception most of the way from South Ferry to 125th Street, due to stray signals from above ground.</p>

<p><strong>Communication breakdown &#8211; The R train</strong></p>

<p>Be prepared for some radio silence when heading into Brooklyn&#8212;you will find almost no reception over there.</p>

<p><strong>Send it while you can &#8211; The L train</strong></p>

<p>Make the most of the strong pockets of reception at 8th Ave and 6th Ave&#8212;no service again before Wilson Ave when heading east.</p>

<p><strong>Connection with a view &#8211; The Q train</strong></p>

<p>Send your messages while crossing the Manhattan Bridge, one of the few well-connected spots on the Q (and, you get a nice view!).</p>
</blockquote>

<figure style="max-width: 620.0px;"><img alt="" src="https://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2016/02/nn/3a02e62c3.jpg"><figcaption class="credit">One type of Subspotting poster.</figcaption></figure><figure style="max-width: 620.0px;"><img alt="" src="https://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2016/02/nnn/b6d0a8df3.jpg"><figcaption class="caption">A selection from another poster showing linear comparisons of the different lines. (<a href="http://subspotting.nyc/main/#poster">Subspotting</a>)</figcaption></figure></div>
