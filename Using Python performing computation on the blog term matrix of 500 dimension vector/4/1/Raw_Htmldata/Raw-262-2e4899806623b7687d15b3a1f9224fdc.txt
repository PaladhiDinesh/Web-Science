		<h1 class="entry-title">The Second Avenue Subway explained</h1>	</header><!-- .entry-header -->

	<div class="entry-content">
		<h6>Written by: REBECCA HARSHBARGER AND CRISTIAN SALAZAR</h6>
<p>The construction of the Second Avenue Subway on the Upper East Side has been an epic odyssey. But after decades of false starts, officials said on Friday that the subway line’s first phase is 90% complete.</p>
<p>The journey to launch the new line goes back to the 1920s, when the route was first proposed by a state agency. Construction has been derailed by the Great Depression, World War II, the city’s financial woes in the seventies, and the challenges of building in one of the densest neighborhoods in the country.</p>
<p>The state finally broke ground on this first phase of the line in April 2007, though construction work had been undertaken in previous decades in Chinatown and East Harlem.</p>
<p>MTA officials say that once it is complete, the Second Avenue Subway will allow residents and workers to be able to get to the west side of Manhattan more easily, and many commuters near Second Avenue may be able to skip bus or car service.</p>
<p>“There are many benefits. It will give the Upper East Side more travel options that they didn’t have,” said board member Andrew Albert, chairman of the Transit Riders Council.</p>
<p>Here’s what else you need to know about the Second Avenue Subway.</p>
<section id="1">
<h2>The Second Avenue Subway is designed to take ridership pressure off the crowded Lexington Line</h2>
<figure><img id="1_11244945" class="" title="The Second Avenue Subway is designed to take ridership pressure off the crowded Lexington Line  " src="https://i0.wp.com/cdn.newsday.com/polopoly_fs/1.11244945.1450645819!/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/display_600/image.jpg" alt="The MTA expects the new subway route to" /></p>
<div class="simpleShare"></div>
</figure>
<p>The MTA expects the new subway route to be used by 200,000 people each weekday, which will take ridership pressure off the Lexington Avenue route, the most-used transit line in the country. Currently, that route, on the Upper East Side, is served by the No. 4, 5, and 6 trains. That&#8217;s in stark contrast to the Upper West Side, which is served by both the Broadway lines (No. 1, 2, and 3 trains) and the Central Park West lines (the B and C trains). The neighborhood once had two other subway routes on Second and Third avenues, but the noisy elevated routes were torn down to improve real estate values.<em>(Credit: Brian via Flickr)</em></p>
</section>
<section id="2">
<h2>There’s a ‘moderate risk’ the MTA won’t be able to meet its Dec. 2016 deadline</h2>
<figure><img id="1_11244944" class="" title="There’s a ‘moderate risk’ the MTA won’t be able to meet its Dec. 2016 deadline " src="https://i1.wp.com/cdn.newsday.com/polopoly_fs/1.11244944.1450484137!/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/display_600/image.jpg" alt="The Second Avenue Subway was designed to be" /></p>
<div class="simpleShare"></div>
</figure>
<p>The Second Avenue Subway was designed to be built in four phases. The $4.45 billion first phase, which is currently under construction, will include three stations on 96th, 86th, and 72nd streets. It is scheduled to open to the public in December 2016. However, the MTA is behind on track installation and construction of entrances at the future 72nd Street station, as well as bringing power to the 86th Street station.<em>(Credit: MTA)</em></p>
</section>
<section id="3">
<h2>No one reading this story will likely be alive when the Second Avenue Subway is completed</h2>
<figure><img id="1_11244941" class="" title="No one reading this story will likely be alive when the Second Avenue Subway is completed" src="https://i0.wp.com/cdn.newsday.com/polopoly_fs/1.11244941.1450484096!/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/display_600/image.jpg" alt="It's unclear when the other three phases will" /></p>
<div class="simpleShare"></div>
</figure>
<p>It&#8217;s unclear when the other three phases will be completed or how much they will cost. There is $535 million budgeted in the 2015-2019 capital plan for design work and utility location for the second phase that runs to East Harlem, with tunneling expected to begin after 2019. The third phase will run to Houston Street, the fourth to the Financial District.</p>
</section>
<section id="4">
<h2>The three new stations will have new features to keep them cool and accessible</h2>
<figure><img id="1_11244939" class="" title="The three new stations will have new features to keep them cool and accessible" src="https://i0.wp.com/cdn.newsday.com/polopoly_fs/1.11244939.1450617579!/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/display_600/image.jpg" alt="While there won't be air conditioning at the" /></p>
<div class="simpleShare"></div>
</figure>
<p>While there won&#8217;t be air conditioning at the new stations, they will be 10 degrees cooler than it is on the street due to climate controls. The stations will all have Wi-Fi and porcelain panels that are easier to clean. They will also be accessible by wheelchair; there will be elevators and escalators. They have an open style and are column-free.<em>(Credit: MTA)</em></p>
</section>
<section id="5">
<h2>The Q train will serve the Second Avenue Subway</h2>
<figure><img id="1_11244938" class="" title="The Q train will serve the Second Avenue Subway" src="https://i0.wp.com/cdn.newsday.com/polopoly_fs/1.11244938.1452035571!/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/display_600/image.jpg" alt="When the Q is sent to the Upper" /></p>
<div class="simpleShare"></div>
</figure>
<p>When the Q is sent to the Upper East Side, instead of Queens, the MTA may bring back the W train that hasn&#8217;t been around since 2010. N train riders who currently have only local service in Manhattan may then get express service back. Local stations along the Broadway line that the N currently serve will likely need another train to make up for the loss of service.<em>(Credit: gmpicket via Flickr)</em></p>
</section>
<section id="6">
<h2>The cost of building the Second Avenue Subway was once estimated at $86 million</h2>
<figure><img id="1_11244926" class="" title="The cost of building the Second Avenue Subway was once estimated at $86 million" src="https://i2.wp.com/cdn.newsday.com/polopoly_fs/1.11244926.1450483342!/httpImage/image.png_gen/derivatives/display_600/image.png" alt="When the Second Avenue Subway was proposed in" /></p>
<div class="simpleShare"></div>
</figure>
<p>When the Second Avenue Subway was proposed in the 1920s, it was estimated it would cost $86 million to build it from the Harlem River to Houston Street. The MTA is spending $4.45 billion on the three new stops, as well as a connection to the Lexington Avenue/63rd Street station.<em>(Credit: MTA)</em></p>
</section>
